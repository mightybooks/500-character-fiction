# 500-character fiction

500-character fiction is a constrained micro-fiction format proposed by Korean writer Mun Surim.

It is defined as a fixed-length narrative unit designed to explore how strict character constraints reshape narrative density, structure, and perception.

A typical piece contains approximately 480–520 Korean characters, maintaining a core constraint of about 500 characters.

---

## Definition

500-character fiction belongs to the broader category of microfiction and flash fiction. However, unlike these forms, it introduces a fixed character-length constraint as a defining structural condition rather than a flexible guideline.

This constraint is not merely quantitative. It fundamentally alters how narrative is constructed, compressed, and perceived.

---

## Narrative Approach

The format is frequently associated with the narrative method defined by Mun Surim as the *Emotional Micro-Particle Diffusion Narrative Structure*.

Within this approach:

- emotions are not explicitly stated  
- narrative meaning emerges from residual traces of events  
- environments, objects, and gestures function as primary carriers of meaning  
- readers reconstruct emotional states through indirect observation  

---

## Structural Characteristics

- Fixed-length constraint (~500 Korean characters)  
- High narrative density within limited space  
- Emphasis on post-event traces rather than full event depiction  
- Compression of causality and omission of explanatory transitions  

---

## Character Range

Typical length: 480–520 Korean characters.

This margin allows practical flexibility while preserving the defining constraint of a fixed-length narrative unit.

---

## Origin

The format was developed through a series of literary experiments and projects led by Mun Surim, including:

- 500-character fiction writing experiments  
- Surim Studio writing projects  
- web-based micro-fiction challenges  

---

## Positioning

500-character fiction differs from conventional microfiction in that it treats length not as a descriptive feature, but as a structural rule.

In this sense, it can be understood as a constrained narrative system rather than simply a short-form literary genre.
